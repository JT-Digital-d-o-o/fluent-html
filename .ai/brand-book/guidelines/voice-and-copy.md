# Voice & Copy

## Brand Voice

JT Digital writes like experienced engineers talking to peers — confident, direct, and opinionated, but never arrogant. We explain decisions, not credentials. We show proof, not promises.

### Voice Attributes

| Attribute | What it means | What it doesn't mean |
|-----------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Direct** | Lead with the point. Short sentences. No filler. | Blunt or dismissive |
| **Confident** | State opinions clearly. Own our choices. | Bragging or overselling |
| **Technical** | Use real terms. Show real code and real numbers. | Jargon for jargon's sake |
| **Honest** | Acknowledge trade-offs. Admit what's hard. | Negative or self-deprecating |
| **Conversational** | Write like you'd explain it to a smart colleague. | Casual or sloppy |

### The JT Digital Tone in Practice

**We say:** "We tried BullMQ first. It had 10-15 second delays in edge cases — unacceptable for live TV. setInterval is self-healing: if one cycle is missed, the next catches it."

**We don't say:** "After careful evaluation of multiple queue-based architectures, our team determined that a simpler polling mechanism would better serve our requirements."

**We say:** "Sometimes the boring solution is the correct one."

**We don't say:** "We leverage industry best practices to deliver optimal solutions."

## Writing Patterns

### Blog Posts

Blog posts follow a consistent structure:

1. **Hook** — Open with a concrete statement, not a question. Lead with what we did or what we believe.
   - "When your app runs live on national television, there's no second chance."
   - "Most agencies send a document after the first meeting. We send something you can click."

2. **Problem/Context** — Frame the challenge honestly. Name the constraints.

3. **Our approach** — Explain what we did and why. Include real code, real metrics, real architecture decisions. Don't hide the trade-offs.

4. **Results** — Close with concrete outcomes. Numbers, not adjectives.

5. **CTA** — End with "Start a Conversation" and a one-line invitation.

### Headlines

- Short. Declarative. No clickbait.
- Prefer statements over questions.
- Good: "Why We Give Clients a Prototype Before a Proposal"
- Good: "Olimpijski Kviz — Building a Live Quiz for National Television"
- Bad: "You Won't Believe How We Built a Quiz App!"
- Bad: "10 Things We Learned About Scaling"

### Service & Product Descriptions

Lead with the client benefit, then ground it:
- "Get exactly what your business needs. We build tailored solutions that solve your unique challenges and grow with you."
- "Launch your SaaS product with confidence. We handle the complexity so you can focus on serving your customers."

Pattern: **[Benefit for you]**. **[How we deliver it]**.

### Formatting Conventions

- Use **bold** for key phrases and terms, not for emphasis on every other word.
- Use `code formatting` for technical terms (stack names, tools, config values).
- Use blockquotes (`>`) for key takeaways or standout lines.
- Use tables and bullet points over long paragraphs.
- Use real numbers: "16ms median response time", not "blazing fast".

## Words We Use

| Use | Instead of |
|-----|-----------|
| build | develop, implement, engineer |
| ship | deliver, deploy, release |
| simple | streamlined, optimized |
| honest | transparent (when describing ourselves) |
| craft | methodology, practice |
| conversation | consultation, discovery call |
| works | performs, functions |

## Words We Avoid

- "Leverage", "utilize", "synergy", "paradigm"
- "World-class", "cutting-edge", "revolutionary"
- "Agile" (as a brand identity — we're agile in practice, we don't sell it)
- "Full-stack" (we're specific about what we use: TypeScript, HTMX, Fastify)
- Buzzwords without substance — if we mention AI, we show what it actually does

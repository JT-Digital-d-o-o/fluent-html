# Loonshots Manifesto

The history of every industry-defining company contains at least one loonshot: a wildly ambitious idea that most people dismissed. The question is never "do we have crazy ideas?" — it's "do we have a structure that keeps them alive long enough to prove themselves?"

---

## Phase Transitions and Organizational Structure

### DO: Treat structure as the root cause of innovation failure

The same people behave completely differently under different structures. A 10-person team explores freely; a 150-person team becomes political. This isn't a culture problem — it's a physics problem. When return-on-politics exceeds return-on-work, people stop nurturing loonshots and start protecting turf.

Ask: "Is our structure rewarding great work, or rewarding the appearance of great work?"

### DON'T: Try to fix innovation failure with culture initiatives

Values posters, off-sites, and innovation sprints don't move the control parameter. They treat the symptom. If career advancement depends on headcount and title rather than outcomes, loonshots will keep dying — regardless of stated values.

---

## S-Type vs. P-Type Loonshots

### DO: Watch for both types of breakthrough

| Type | What it is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **P-type** (Product) | A technology or feature most people believe won't work | Radar, statins, the transistor |
| **S-type** (Strategy) | A business model or go-to-market most people believe won't scale | Walmart in small towns, frequent flyer miles, Amazon Prime |

Most teams only protect P-type ideas (new features, new tech). S-type loonshots — a new pricing model, a different customer segment, a counterintuitive distribution strategy — die just as easily and matter just as much.

### DON'T: Kill strategy experiments before they have data

S-type loonshots look stupid before they work. "Loyalty points for flights" looked like an accounting gimmick. "Free two-day shipping subscription" looked like a money-loser. Give strategy experiments the same protected space as product experiments.

## The Loonshot Nursery

### DO: Separate artists from soldiers

Two groups must coexist without either consuming the other:

- **Loonshot nursery** — the team (or time, or budget) dedicated to exploring high-risk ideas that aren't ready for execution. Artists live here. Their job is to keep ideas alive through the valley of death.
- **Franchise** — the team executing on what's already proven, shipping product, hitting metrics. Soldiers live here. Their job is to be reliable.

The nursery needs protection from the franchise's efficiency demands. The franchise needs protection from the nursery's chaos.

### DON'T: Let the franchise set the agenda for the nursery

When the execution team controls prioritization for the exploration team, only safe, near-term ideas survive. Nursery output gets pruned to what's "realistic" — which is precisely what kills loonshots.

---

## Dynamic Equilibrium

### DO: Create active feedback between nursery and franchise

Separation without connection produces irrelevance. The nursery invents; the franchise field-tests and reports back what actually works with real customers. This feedback loop — called the **transfer mechanism** — is what turns loonshots into products.

Structure the transfer: regular, structured reviews where nursery ideas get tested in the field, and field results inform nursery priorities. Neither group should be allowed to ignore the other.

### DON'T: Let the transfer become a one-way gate

If ideas only flow from nursery to franchise (but learnings never flow back), the nursery loses signal and starts optimizing for novelty over relevance. Transfer is a two-way channel.

---

## The False Fail

### DO: Investigate failures before declaring an idea dead

A loonshot that "failed" in the field usually failed because of **execution**, not because of the underlying idea. This is the **false fail**: the idea was right, but the rollout was wrong — wrong customer, wrong timing, wrong framing, wrong champion.

Before killing a loonshot, ask: "Did the idea fail, or did the first implementation of the idea fail?" These are very different decisions.

### DON'T: Let a single failed experiment kill a thesis

One failed MVP does not invalidate the hypothesis. Look for false fails — cases where the experiment was poorly designed, the wrong customer was targeted, or the idea was handed to someone who didn't believe in it.

---

## Listen to the Suck with Curiosity (LSC)

### DO: Treat early negative feedback as signal, not verdict

Every loonshot that eventually succeeded went through a "valley of death" where the feedback was uniformly bad. The people closest to the technology or strategy heard "this will never work" from people who didn't understand it yet. The correct response is curiosity: "Why exactly does this suck? What would need to change for this not to suck?"

### DON'T: Respond to negative feedback with either dismissal or despair

- **Dismissal** ("they just don't get it") closes the feedback loop and prevents learning.
- **Despair** ("they're right, let's kill it") throws away potentially valid ideas on insufficient evidence.

The right response is structured investigation: what specifically failed, in what context, for what reason?

---

## Return on Work vs. Return on Politics

### DO: Make career advancement depend on outcomes, not visibility

The single most powerful structural lever is how you reward people. If the path to promotion runs through great results, people chase great results. If it runs through managing up and accumulating reports, people chase politics. Design compensation, promotion, and recognition around measurable outcomes.

### DON'T: Promote based on seniority or headcount managed

Every promotion of a politically skilled but low-output person shifts the incentive calculus. The team learns what actually gets rewarded. Promote people who shipped things that worked, who kept ideas alive that mattered, and who gave honest feedback when an idea wasn't working.

## Integration with Lean Startup and Shape Up

### DO: Use Loonshots to decide what experiments are worth running

| Phase | Framework | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Wild idea, unknown potential | **Loonshots** | Protect it. Put it in the nursery. Don't kill it with ROI math yet. |
| Hypothesis formed, ready to test | **Lean Startup** | Run a fast experiment. Build-Measure-Learn in 1–3 days. |
| Validated, ready to ship | **Shape Up** | Set appetite. Shape it. Bet on it. Ship within the cycle. |

Loonshots answers "which crazy bets deserve a protected space to breathe?" Lean Startup answers "how do we test them efficiently?" Shape Up answers "how do we execute once we know it works?"

### DON'T: Apply Shape Up's appetite constraint to nursery exploration

A loonshot in early-stage exploration has no business being time-boxed to a 1-week appetite. Constraints are for execution, not for keeping ideas alive through the valley of death. Protect the nursery from execution-phase thinking until the idea has earned its first validation signal.

# Progress Tracking: Hill Charts and Showing Progress

Good managers don't like asking for status — it's awkward and feels like nagging. Instead, create a system where status is visible without interrupting the team. The hill chart shifts focus from what's done or not done to what's unknown and what's solved.

---

## Why To-Dos Aren't Enough

### The tasks that aren't there

To-do lists actually grow as the team makes progress. A scope with all tasks done might mean it's finished — or it might mean new tasks haven't been discovered yet. You can't judge from the outside whether outstanding tasks will go up or down.

### Estimates don't show uncertainty

Two tasks both estimated at four hours have very different meanings. One is something the team has done ten times (confident). The other has unclear interdependencies and could stretch to three days. Writing "4 hours, or maybe 3 days" isn't meaningful.

---

## The Hill Metaphor

Every piece of work has two phases:

| Phase | Character | Feeling |
|---|---|---|
| **Uphill** | Figuring out approach, unknowns, problem-solving | "I'm still not sure what I'm doing" |
| **Downhill** | Execution, certainty, confidence | "Now I know what to do" |

At the top of the hill, you can see all remaining steps. It's even fair to estimate time from here. Below the hill on the left, estimation doesn't make sense because you haven't figured out the approach yet.

---

## Hill Charts in Practice

Plot each scope as a dot on a hill diagram. The position shows where the work stands on the unknown → known → done spectrum.

### DO: Update the hill chart regularly

Team members with full context drag scopes into position. The update is logged as a snapshot.

### DO: Compare past states

The killer feature for managers: seeing not just where work stands but how it's moving. A sequence of snapshots reveals which problems the team chose to solve and how much time they spent at each stage.

### DO: Use the dot that doesn't move as a signal

A dot that doesn't move is effectively a raised hand: "Something might be wrong here." The hill chart lets everybody see that someone might be stuck without them actually having to say it.

---

## Build Your Way Uphill

### DON'T: Just think about the approach — validate with your hands

Coming up with an approach in your head is just the first step. "I'll just use that API" often turns out more complicated in reality.

### Three stages of going uphill

| Position | Meaning |
|---|---|
| First third | "I've thought about this" |
| Second third | "I've validated my approach" |
| Final third (top) | "I'm far enough with what I've built that I don't believe there are other unknowns" |

---

## Conversations About Progress

### DO: Use the language of uphill/downhill

It's less about the person ("Looks like you're stuck!") and more about the work ("What can we solve to get that over the hill?"). This makes it safe for people to surface problems.

### Nobody says "I don't know"

Nobody wants to raise their hand and say "I don't know how to solve this." Teams hide uncertainty and accumulate risk. The hill chart makes stuck work visible without requiring anyone to admit it.

### DO: Jump directly into the relevant piece of work

"Looks like 'Autosave' has been uphill for a while. What's the unknown that's holding it back?" The manager can workshop this specific piece without untangling it from everything else.

---

## Refactoring Scopes

When a scope appears stuck, the problem might be in how the scope is drawn, not in the work itself.

### DO: Break stuck scopes into smaller scopes

A "Notify" scope that was stuck turned out to be three things: designing an email, delivering the email, and displaying an in-app notification. The team had mostly finished delivery but hadn't started the display. It was impossible to say whether "Notify" was over the hill because parts were and parts weren't.

After splitting into three scopes, each could move independently and show more frequent progress.

---

## Solve in the Right Sequence

### DO: Push the scariest work uphill first

If you're out of time at the end of the cycle, which scope could you whip together despite unknowns, and which might prove harder than expected? Do the risky one first.

### DO: Follow the "inverted pyramid" from journalism

Most essential information first. Get critical scopes over the top early, leave routine for last.

### DON'T: Start with the easy work

Work expands to fill available time. If you start with the email template, you could spend days iterating on copy. But a version could be worked out in an hour on the final day. The novel, scary problem — that's the one you don't want to surprise you at the end.

As the end of the cycle approaches, the important things should be done and only "nice-to-haves" and "maybes" remain.

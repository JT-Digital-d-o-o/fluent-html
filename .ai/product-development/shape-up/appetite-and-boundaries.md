# Appetite and Boundaries: Fixed Time, Variable Scope

Instead of asking "How long will this take?" ask "How much time do we want to spend?" The appetite is a time budget that acts as a creative constraint — it determines which solution you design, not whether you can finish a predetermined scope.

---

## Appetite vs. Estimate

| | Appetite | Estimate |
|---|---|---|
| Starts with | A number (time budget) | A design (scope) |
| Ends with | A design that fits | A number that might not |
| Controls | What kind of solution to design | Nothing — just predicts |
| Scope | Variable | Fixed |

### DO: Set the appetite before designing the solution

The appetite constrains what kind of solution you design. A 1-week appetite leads to a different solution than a 2-day appetite. Both can be "good" — good is relative to constraints.

### DON'T: Estimate first, then try to fit the schedule

This leads to fixed scope with variable time — the root cause of projects that drag on and on.

---

## Two Standard Sizes

- **Small Batch**: 1-2 days of work for a team of one designer and one or two programmers. Multiple small batch projects are bundled into a single one-week cycle.
- **Big Batch**: A full week for the same-size team. One project, one team, one cycle.

If a project doesn't fit in one week, don't extend the time. Either narrow the problem definition or break off a meaningful part that can be shaped to a one-week appetite.

---

## Fixed Time, Variable Scope

This is the key principle that makes everything work. When time is fixed, you must make decisions about scope. Without a deadline, you'd never make the trade-off between fixing typos and adding a new section. Without variable scope, you'd have to include everything and blow the deadline.

### DO: Apply fixed time, variable scope at every stage

- During **shaping**: the appetite constrains what kind of solution you design
- During **building**: the fixed time box pushes the team to decide what's core versus peripheral

### DON'T: Treat scope as fixed and let time expand

This is how projects become death marches. If scope isn't variable, the team can't reconsider design decisions that cost more than they're worth.

---

## Responding to Raw Ideas

### DO: Default to "Interesting. Maybe some day."

A very soft "no" that leaves all options open. Don't put it in a backlog. Give it space to learn whether it's really important.

### DON'T: Say "yes" or "no" on first contact

Even exciting ideas need shaping before committing. Showing too much enthusiasm sets expectations. Saying "yes" to everything creates an ever-growing pile of obligations.

### DO: Keep a cool manner and a poker face

New information might come tomorrow that changes how you see an idea. Don't shut down what you don't understand, but don't set expectations either.

---

## Narrowing the Problem

Before you can shape a solution, you need a specific problem — not a vague request.

### DO: Ask "when" not "why"

When did the thought to ask for this occur? What were you doing? What was the specific workflow that broke down? This gives you a concrete baseline to design against.

### DO: Flip from "What could we build?" to "What's really going wrong?"

A customer asking for "a calendar" isn't a problem statement. "I need to see free spaces so I can schedule something" is a problem you can solve in one week.

### DON'T: Accept grab-bags as projects

"Redesign the Files section" is a grab-bag, not a project. It's impossible to figure out where it starts and ends. The "2.0" label is a tell-tale sign of a grab-bag.

### DO: Start from a specific pain point

"We need to rethink the Files section because sharing multiple files takes too many steps." Now you can ask what's not working, what can stay the same, and what needs to change.

---

## Boundaries in Place

You're ready to move to the next step — finding the elements of a solution — when you have all three things:

1. **A raw idea** — the starting point
2. **An appetite** — how much time it's worth
3. **A narrow problem definition** — the specific pain point to solve

If you can't figure out a specific pain point, your appetite tells you how much research is worthwhile. If it's not critical now and you can't get your hands around the problem, walk away and work on something else.
